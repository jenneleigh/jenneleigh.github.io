    Sigma Color Refresh

    A deep dive into the process of updating, streamlining, expanding and documenting Sigma's product colors

    Background

    When I joined, Sigma had just finished a major rebranding. However, the design system was in a poor state and the updated colors scheme had not been added to the product consistently.

    Additionally, accessibility color contrasts were all over the map and there was no consistent rules for the usage and application of the individual colors.

    Luckily, when presented with this data, our Creative Director and Senior Visual Designer were onboard with making some changes.

    Finding a new blue

    Our first plan attack was to continue using our new brand colors with adjusted color scales and tweaking the teal alternative to be a more own-able color.

    I worked with my team to test out a variety of new blues (and a couple purples). As you can probably tell, there was a lot of back and forth

    Finally we landed on an option that even the pickiest among us could agree upon and I began developing a color scales for the rest of our brand colors.

    Plot twist!

    But then, an imminent product launch threw a wrench in the works. Sigma was a few weeks away from publicly launching Workbooks, a major new feature that completely rethought how our product functioned.

    One of the major selling points of Workbooks is that it allows our customers to analyze their data and then seamlessly pass along the results to their customers. However, that also meant they needed a lot of control over the visuals and that did not include Sigma's branding.

    So now trying to strengthen the presence of our brand in the product was no longer ideal. So we went back to the drawing board

    Regroup

    We needed a new strategy. Luckily, our Web Infra team was looking into the process of rebuilding our component library with Radix and they pointed me to their excellent color system.

    I realized that, with a little tweaking, it suited our needs perfectly.

    The New System

    The new plan was this:

    • A revised version of the radix scale was created to provide semantic colors that would be effectively "unbranded". Each color was assigned a meaning and each step had specific use cases.
    • And a revised and streamlined version of the expanded brand palette was created to use for things like illustrations and accents colors.

    I documented each color and the use cases in Figma and created a separate document for designers and engineers that showed how the new colors mapped onto the old ones.

    Final Results

    The new color system was well received, especially when we showed off the improved color numbers.

    We did find that the number of color options was a bit overwhelming for our team, so we trimmed it down the number of steps for each color except gray, with the understanding that we could reintroduce the other steps when the need arose.

    Next Steps

    Really, a project like this will probably never be truly "final". As I write this, I have just successfully argued to reintroduce the cute colors (along with the promise to further clarify the use cases for each step.

    We're also adding several other colors from the Radix system for our users to choose from and define their own semantic meaning as part of our Version Tagging feature.

    At the request of several engineers, I am also going to be expanding our semantic usage by adding more semantic color tokens to our system. This should also help ease the amount that the 12 color steps overwhelms our designers.
